\subsection{NVE Ensemble Results}

The implementation details for the NVE molecular dynamics simulation are provided in Appendix~\ref{ref:app:MD 1 NVE}.

The evolution of energy over time is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig: energy tau}. An initial equilibration period is observed during approximately the first 300 time steps, after which the system stabilizes.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{graphs/P2E1/Energy_tau.png}
	\caption{Evolution of total, kinetic, and potential energy over time.}
	\label{fig: energy tau}
\end{figure}

To determine an optimal time step ($dt$), we performed a series of simulations across a range of $dt$ values. The stability of each simulation was assessed by calculating the standard deviation of the total energy over time, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig: energy dt}.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{graphs/P2E1/Energy_dt.png}
	\caption{Standard deviation of total energy as a function of time step size.}
	\label{fig: energy dt}
\end{figure}

The data indicates that for $dt > 10^{-2}$, the simulation becomes unstable and the results are non-physical. While the energy fluctuation appears to plateau for values below $10^{-2}$—suggesting that smaller steps do not significantly improve the description of the dynamics—subsequent testing revealed occasional energy jumps at $dt = 10^{-2}$. Thus, a more conservative time step of $dt = 10^{-3}$ was selected for all future simulations to ensure long-term numerical stability.

\subsection{Theoretical Expectations}

According to the thermodynamic equipartition theorem, the internal energy of a system with $3N$ translational degrees of freedom is given by:
\begin{equation}
	E_{trans} = \frac{3}{2} N \beta^{-1}
\end{equation}

For a system interacting via a Lennard-Jones potential, additional degrees of freedom may arise from the potential wells. In a dense system, the radial oscillations of particles around their equilibrium positions can contribute further quadratic terms to the Hamiltonian. In the limit of a harmonic crystal, this would add another $\frac{1}{2} N \beta^{-1}$; however, for a fluid where particles are partially confined, we expect a smaller contribution. If we assume the potential adds at most one additional quadratic degree of freedom, the total energy would follow $E_t = \frac{4}{2} N \beta^{-1}$.

Because the system is not at an ideal packing density, not all particles occupy the harmonic (quadratic) region of the potential well. We can therefore model the total energy as:
\begin{equation}
	E_t = \frac{3 + \gamma(\rho)}{2} N \beta^{-1}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma(\rho) \in [0, 1]$ is a density-dependent parameter representing the fraction of particles effectively trapped within the quadratic region of the potential.

By applying a linear fit to the energy-temperature data, we obtained a slope of $434$, which corresponds to:
\begin{equation}
	E_t \approx \frac{3.4}{2} N \beta^{-1}
\end{equation}
This result suggests that $\gamma(\rho) \approx 0.4$, implying that approximately $40\%$ of the particles are situated within the potential minima at this specific density.

\subsection{Velocity-Velocity Correlation Function}

The velocity autocorrelation function (VACF) is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	C_v(t) = \langle \mathbf{v}(0) \cdot \mathbf{v}(t) \rangle
\end{equation}

Initial results for the VACF calculated over a single simulation run are presented in Figure~\ref{fig: correlation}. As can be seen there are a lot of fluctuations.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{graphs/P2E1/example time.png}
	\caption{Velocity autocorrelation function for a single simulation run, showing high statistical noise.}
	\label{fig: correlation}
\end{figure}

To improve the signal-to-noise ratio, the simulation was repeated 100 times. The average of these independent trajectories is shown in Figure~\ref{fig: average correlation}.

\begin{figure}[H]

\subsection{Diffusion Coefficient}

The self-diffusion coefficient $D$ can be calculated from the velocity autocorrelation function (VACF) using the Green-Kubo relation:
\begin{equation}
	D = \frac{1}{3} \int_{0}^{\infty} \langle \mathbf{v}(0) \cdot \mathbf{v}(t) \rangle \, dt
\end{equation}
where the factor of $1/3$ corresponds to the three translational degrees of freedom in our system ($d=3$).

In practice, the upper limit of the integral is replaced by a correlation time $\tau_{max}$ at which the VACF has sufficiently decayed to approximately zero. Integrating the averaged correlation function shown in Figure~\ref{fig: average correlation}, we obtain:
\begin{equation*}
	D \approx 0.24 \quad
\end{equation*}
